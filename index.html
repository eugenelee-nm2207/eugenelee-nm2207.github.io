Introduction

Let's start with something simple: What is your gender?

In his survey, Lafan (2020) conducted a survey on 1477 individuals who identified as Kpop-fans.

An overwhelming majority of current Kpop fans are female (70.2%). Historically, it has been said that male Kpop groups tend to do better, because they appeal to a much larger demographic of Kpop fans.

In fact, female fans have also been said to be more dedicated fans who are more willing to invest time and money, in particular by purchasing albums. Hence, traditionally, it was assumed that male groups dominated physical album sales, while female groups tended to match up better in online streaming and downloads (Yoon, 2022) by appealing to the general public.

Album sales are one of the main avenues through which Kpop idol groups make profits, and generates much more revenue than online streams. This has put male groups further ahead in terms of success. To further exacerbate the divide and gender inequality, the types of marketing strategies companies started to use for women in Kpop, caused them to face limits to their autonomy, become over-sexualized, and get compartmentalized into gender roles, in order to appeal to the male gaze (Jonas, 2021).

Fortunately, this issue has been brought to light, and perhaps the tides are starting to turn. Increasingly more so today, Kpop fans have become more vocal in fighting for the rights of the female idols they support. Interestingly, what female groups have gained is not just empowerment, but also a new marketing strategy to do better in the industry. These Kpop girl groups have begun targeting the newer generation, with concepts emphasizing, and even commodifying, the theme of female empowerment and breaking stereotypes (Sun, Paje & Lee, 2021; to Zhang et al., 2022).

Has this strategy been successful? Are male Kpop groups still more likely to be more successful? My data story will endeavour to make a preliminary, exploratory study on this.

I will approach this question from 3 domains that contribute to a Kpop groups' success: physical album sales, digital success, as well as brand reputation. Let's get straight to it!


Physical Sales

Since the main advantage that male idols are said to have are in terms of physical album sales, we will first evaluate if it is true.

When we observe the 54 albums by Kpop groups with the highest sales in South Korea right now, we see that an overwhelming majority are produced by male groups (43 male versus 11 female).

A closer look at the data tells us that many of the albums are made by the same groups, like BTS, who owns the top 4 best selling albums as well. What a powerhouse!üò± When we filter this dataset such that each group only has one unique album, the distribution becomes more even (9 male vs 8 female). The average sales of the top 8 male albums is 2,776,510, while that of female albums is significantly far behind, at 1,455,182.

This disparity is as jarring when we observe album sales by group. The best selling female group, TWICE, is only in 8th position, behind 7 other male groups.

Therefore, it appears that male groups are really leaps and bounds ahead of female groups in terms of physical album sales.


Digital Success

Okay, so we're off to a bad start üò™. Now that we have clarified the disparity in physical sales, let's analyze the digital success of Kpop groups. In this study, I will approach this from two angles: Google searches and Youtube Video views.

First, let's have a look at Google searches, which is one indicator of the popularity of a group. The more often you are searched, the greater the presence you have among audiences. The 8 countries below ‚Äì 3 Western and 5 Asian ‚Äì are those with some of the greatest number of Kpop audiences.

Okay, so what? Let's visualize the information on a map to have a better view of the data. Countries with greater female group popularity appear more red, and those with greater male group populairty appear more blue.

The greatest possible proportion of female groups in the top 5 searches of any country is only 60%, while that of male groups is at 80%. It appears that there are more countries where male groups are more popular, in both Western countries and in Asia.

How about Youtube Views? Youtube is one of the main platforms where Kpop idols, and artists across the world, present their products, and even make money from streams and advertisements.

Let's have a look at the scatter chart, which maps the top 51 most-viewed Youtube videos by unique Kpop groups, the number of streams per video, and each video's number of daily increases in views.

As usual, BTS and BLACKPINK have a staggeringly larger number of views on their music videos (MVs) versus other groups. As for the other groups, there seems to be a generally similar distribution, though there are slightly more female groups with a higher number of daily increases in views.

To better compare the data, we can compare the averages of the male and female data. At 301 million average total views and 130,429 daily increases, male average values appear significantly lower than those of females, at 314 million total views and 201,818 daily increases.

Overall, for the domain of digital presence, female groups tend to do better on YouTube, though on Google searches, male groups are doing better. How do we make sense of this? While female groups do not seem to perform exceedingly better in digital presence, we have found evidence that they are at least not falling behind too greatly, which was the case for physical sales. Male groups' larger number of more committed fans may also be helping to boost their Youtube streams and Google searches. On the other hand, female groups may still do better in YouTube because they can draw in a larger number of viewers from the general public.


Branding

The third domain is that of brand reputation. I have managed to obtain the Brand Reputation Index of Kpop groups in February 2023.

This index is constructed using big data by the Korean Business Research Institute, and ‚Äúrankings are determined by collecting data on consumer participation, media coverage, interaction, and community indexes of K-Pop groups over the last month‚Äù (Koreaboo, 2023)(not the best website to cite from, I know, but what better place is there to get facts about Kpop?ü§°). Hence, it is a more general measure of the popularity of Kpop groups, among current audiences.

Of the 100 most popular artists, I have extracted the data for just Kpop groups. Let's have a look at the chart:

Evidently, there are many more female groups (17 female to 6 male) who have ranked in the top 100. Furthermore, there are quite a number of female groups amongst the top few rankings. This brings us back to the general trend where female groups may tend to do better among the general public. In particular, they have more media coverage, which may also indicate greater digital success outside of Google searches and YouTube.

Brand reputation may also point to the visibility of a group, and their level of activities in brand endorsements, which is also a major source of revenue. Even if female groups may not sell as many physical albums, they could possibly be earning as much, or more profits, via brand deals.

While we have found that the trends in physical sales and digital presence for Kpop groups is mostly consistent with prior findings, to better evaluate if up-and-coming female groups are gaining more success with their images of female empowerment, we can perform a holistic analysis of popular groups today. The most prominent difference, is in the ages of the groups. On average, the female groups have only been around for 2.4 years, a stark contrast from male groups, at 10.6 years.

This could mean many things. Male groups could have more committed, perhaps with a greater proportion of female, fans that stick around to keep the groups' popularity up in the long-term. Meanwhile female groups may also achieve high popularity, but are more short-lived and quickly replaced by other female groups. Alternatively, it could mean that the new strategy of marketing themselves as empowered women, is working. Indeed, all 5 female groups here promote values of confidence and independence, albeit in different ways.

Anyhow, we see that trends identified in earlier sections still remain largely true. On average, female groups are doing much better in brand reputation, while male groups are much more ahead in terms of album sales. There does not appear to be a significant difference in terms of YouTube video streams.


Limitations and Recommendations

It is important to note that this data story is only a preliminary, exploratory venture into the research topic. There are multiple limitations:

Limitations Recommendations
Lack of official datasets.
Unlike other topics, there is no large publicly available dataset which collates information on Kpop groups' performance over multiple domains. Data had to be manually drawn from various sources online, some more credible than others.		 Future work could improve on the study by using larger datasets with more Kpop groups. This will improve reliability, accuracy and generalizability of analyses.
Kpop idols comprise both soloists and groups, the former of which does take up a significant proportion. Only kpop groups were analyzed in this study, due to time and resource constraint, and because amongst solo artists, there is a larger grey area between those who belong to ‚ÄúKpop‚Äù and those who are categorized otherwise, for example as "trot" singers. Future work could develop a method to differentiate between soloists, and include them in analyses, so as to get more accurate findings of trends.
The success of Kpop groups can be determined by many more domains besides the 3 identified in this study. To gain a more complete view of the research question, other domains can be studied, including:
  • Revenue generated: this is a more obvious factor to compare groups' success, though actual values will be difficult to obtain.
  • Actual level of brand endorsements
  • Brand reputation index data over a longer period of time
  • Social media analytics (eg likes and dislikes ratio), on more platforms besides Google and YouTube.
The success of Kpop groups could vary across different regions. Possibilities for future work:
  • Data outside of Korea (i.e for album sales and brand reputation index) could be used.
  • Regional comparisons can be made for domains besides Google searches.

Conclusion

A Kpop group's success is multi-faceted and reliant on many different domains. Male groups may do better in terms of physical album sales, but female groups are ahead in brand reputation. However, there is some indication that newer female groups today, with images of female empowerment, are excelling, and could potentially catch up with male groups in physical sales, or even exceed male groups in the domains other than brand reputation as well.

In the greater scheme of things, this is just one way in which society is moving towards achieving gender equality, at least in the entertainment industry. Women are becoming empowered, and the public is increasingly supportive of their endeavours. As Kpop becomes a more popular phenomenon, and a bigger industry, in the world, more studies on gender equality within the sector is imperative.
